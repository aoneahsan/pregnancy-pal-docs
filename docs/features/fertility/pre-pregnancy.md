# Before pregnancy

> **Pre-pregnancy use** of PregnancyPal means using the app while you're trying to conceive — combining the Period and Fertility features to track your cycle and body signals, and joining the Trying-to-Conceive community cohort for support. There is no separate "trying-to-conceive" mode to switch on; you simply use the cycle-tracking tools the app already provides.

Plenty of people open PregnancyPal before there's a pregnancy to track. The same features that help you understand your cycle — periods, flow, temperature, cervical mucus, ovulation tests — are exactly what's useful when you're trying to conceive. This page explains how to put them together. It is organizational support for your own attention to your cycle, not fertility treatment and not a replacement for the care a clinician provides.

## What you can do before pregnancy

- **Track your cycle** in the [Period feature](../period/cycle-tracking) — log periods, flow intensity, symptoms, and notes.
- **Read estimates** of your ovulation date and fertile window from your logged cycles.
- **Log daily body signals** in the [Fertility feature](./conception-window) — temperature, cervical mucus, ovulation-test results, and symptoms.
- **Watch your fertile window, phase, and fertility score** update from the data you enter.
- **Join the Trying-to-Conceive cohort** in the community to connect with others in the same stage.
- **Keep one record** you can carry into pregnancy if and when it begins, and bring to appointments.

## How to set up PregnancyPal for trying to conceive

1. **Start logging periods** in the Period feature so the app has cycle history to work from.
2. **Open the Fertility feature** and log today's data — temperature, cervical mucus, and any ovulation test.
3. **Check the read-out** at the top of Fertility: estimated ovulation ("Today" / "In X days" / "X days ago"), the fertile-window range, your current phase, and the fertility score.
4. **Keep both updated daily** where you can — the estimates and trends sharpen with consistent logging.
5. **Join the Trying-to-Conceive (ttc) cohort** from the [communities list](../community/joining-cohorts) for peer support.
6. **Remember there's no mode to flip** — using these features consistently *is* the pre-pregnancy setup.

## Examples

- **A combined routine** — Each morning you log your temperature and cervical mucus in Fertility; when a period arrives, you log it in Period. Over a couple of cycles, the fertile-window estimate and the temperature trend start telling a consistent story.
- **Leaning on the cohort** — You join the Trying-to-Conceive community to ask questions and read others' experiences, while keeping your own private data in the Period and Fertility features.
- **Carrying the record forward** — When a pregnancy begins, the cycle history you've built is already in your account, ready to inform the pregnancy side of the app and your first appointments.

## Tips

- **Use both features together.** Period gives the calendar picture; Fertility adds the daily body signals. Together they're more informative than either alone.
- **Consistency beats intensity.** Logging a little each day over several cycles helps far more than a burst of detail for a week.
- **Don't expect a special TTC dashboard.** The cycle tools are the toolkit — there's no hidden mode that unlocks more.
- **Let the community be support, not advice.** The Trying-to-Conceive cohort is peers sharing experiences, not clinicians.
- **Loop in a provider early.** Preconception checkups, prenatal vitamins, and questions about timing are all worth a real conversation.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is there a "trying to conceive" mode I need to enable?

No. PregnancyPal has no dedicated TTC mode toggle. You use the Period and Fertility features as they are, plus the Trying-to-Conceive community cohort. That combination is the whole pre-pregnancy experience.

### Which features should I use while trying to conceive?

The [Period feature](../period/cycle-tracking) for cycle and flow, and the [Fertility feature](./conception-window) for temperature, cervical mucus, ovulation tests, phase, fertile window, and the fertility score. The Trying-to-Conceive cohort adds peer support.

### Can PregnancyPal tell me the best day to conceive?

It can show you an estimated fertile window and ovulation day from your data, which many people use to focus their attention. But these are estimates, not guarantees, and conception depends on many factors beyond timing.

### Can the app tell me if I have a fertility problem?

No. PregnancyPal does not diagnose infertility or any fertility condition. If you've been trying for a while without success, or have any concern, that's a conversation for your healthcare provider.

### How long should I track before talking to a provider?

That's a clinical question, and guidance varies by age and circumstances. The app won't decide it for you — please ask your provider, who can give advice based on your situation.

### What happens to my pre-pregnancy data once I'm pregnant?

It stays in your account. The cycle and fertility history you've logged remains available, and the app's pregnancy features pick up from there.

### Is the Trying-to-Conceive cohort private?

It's a community space where posts are attributed to your display name — there's no anonymous posting. Share at your own comfort level. See [posting and visibility](../community/posting-and-anonymous) for details.

## What this feature does NOT do

- **It does not diagnose infertility** or any reproductive condition. Difficulty conceiving has many possible causes that only a clinician can investigate.
- **It does not provide fertility treatment, medication guidance, or a clinical plan.** It organizes your tracking; it doesn't direct your care.
- **It does not guarantee conception.** Following the estimated fertile window does not ensure pregnancy.
- **It has no contraception logic** and must not be used to avoid pregnancy.
- **It does not replace preconception or fertility care**, and it does not alert you to seek help.

**PregnancyPal is a tracking and organization companion, not medical advice and not a substitute for professional care. If you're planning a pregnancy, struggling to conceive, or have any concern about your reproductive health, please consult your healthcare provider — only a qualified clinician can assess and guide fertility care.**
