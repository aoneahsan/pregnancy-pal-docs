# Ovulation prediction

> **Ovulation prediction** in PregnancyPal is a statistical estimate of the day you may ovulate and the range of days in your fertile window, calculated from the cycles you log. It is an estimate built from your own data — not a measurement of ovulation and not a diagnosis.

When you log periods over time, PregnancyPal looks at the pattern in your data and projects forward: roughly when ovulation might fall and which days form your estimated fertile window. You'll see these in the Cycle Overview widget and reflected as colored phases on the Calendar. They can be genuinely helpful for planning — but they are predictions, and predictions are only as good as the regularity of your cycles and the consistency of your logging.

## What you can do with ovulation prediction

- **See an estimated ovulation date** in the Cycle Overview widget alongside your current cycle info.
- **See an estimated fertile window** — a range of days around that estimate.
- **Spot the phases on the Calendar**, where fertile and prediction days are colored within the month view.
- **Improve the estimate over time** by logging more cycles, since the projection is rebuilt from your accumulated history.
- **Cross-check with real signals** by pairing predictions with temperature and other fertility data in the Fertility feature.

## How to get better ovulation estimates

1. **Log every period's start date** on the Period feature, since the start date anchors the calculation.
2. **Keep logging across several cycles** so the app has a pattern to work from, not a single data point.
3. **Record flow and symptoms** to keep your history complete and your statistics meaningful.
4. **Open the Cycle Overview widget** to read the current estimated ovulation date and fertile window.
5. **Check the Calendar tab** to see those estimated phases laid out across the month.
6. **For a fuller picture, add real measurements** — log temperature and cervical mucus in the [Fertility feature](../fertility/conception-window), which can corroborate or contradict a calendar estimate.

## Examples

- **Regular cycles** — Someone whose periods arrive at a steady interval logs several in a row. The estimated ovulation date and fertile window land in a consistent place each month, and the Calendar's colored phases line up cycle to cycle.
- **Irregular cycles** — Someone with cycles that vary widely from month to month sees the estimate shift more and the fertile window feel less certain. The app still projects a window, but it's a looser guess, and pairing it with temperature data helps.
- **Sparse logging** — After logging only one or two cycles, the estimate exists but is thin. As more periods are logged, the projection settles and becomes more trustworthy.

## Tips

- **Regularity drives accuracy.** The steadier your cycles, the more reliable the estimate; very irregular cycles make any calendar-based prediction shaky.
- **More cycles beat more precision.** Logging consistently over several months helps far more than trying to be exact about a single day.
- **Treat the window, not the single day, as the useful part.** A fertile window acknowledges uncertainty better than one pinpoint date.
- **Confirm with body signals if it matters.** Temperature trends and cervical-mucus observations in the Fertility feature give you real-time signals to weigh against the calendar.
- **Don't use it to avoid pregnancy.** Calendar estimates are not a contraceptive method (see below).

## Frequently asked questions

### How does PregnancyPal estimate my ovulation date?

It derives the estimate from the cycles you log — your recorded period start dates and the pattern they form. It's a calculation based on your historical data, not a sensor reading or a hormone test.

### How accurate is the prediction?

Accuracy depends heavily on how regular your cycles are and how consistently you log. Regular cycles with steady logging produce a tighter, more dependable estimate; irregular cycles or gaps in logging widen the uncertainty. No calendar-based estimate can be exact for everyone.

### Can I rely on this to prevent pregnancy?

No. Ovulation prediction here is not a contraception method and must not be used as one. Ovulation can shift, and the estimate can be wrong. If you want to prevent pregnancy, talk to your healthcare provider about reliable contraception.

### Can I rely on this to get pregnant?

It can help you focus attention on likely days, but it's a guide, not a guarantee. Many factors affect conception. For real-time signals, combine it with temperature and cervical-mucus tracking in the Fertility feature, and speak with a provider if you have questions about fertility.

### Why did my fertile window move?

The window is recalculated from your data. New logged cycles, changes in cycle length, or irregular timing can all shift the estimate. That movement reflects your data, not an error.

### What if I have very irregular cycles?

Calendar-based estimates are least reliable for irregular cycles, because there's no steady pattern to project from. The app will still show a window, but you should treat it loosely and consider tracking physical signals — or talking to a provider about what's behind the irregularity.

### Does ovulation prediction know if I actually ovulated?

No. It estimates when ovulation might occur; it does not detect or confirm that ovulation happened. Only appropriate testing or a clinician can do that.

## What this feature does NOT do

- **It does not measure ovulation.** It estimates a likely date and window from logged-cycle data; it does not detect, confirm, or test for ovulation.
- **It is not a diagnosis.** It cannot tell you whether your cycles are healthy, whether you ovulate, or whether anything is wrong.
- **It is not contraception.** Never use the estimated fertile window or "safe" days to prevent pregnancy.
- **It is not a fertility guarantee.** Timing intercourse to the estimate does not ensure conception.
- **It does not account for medical conditions, medications, or hormonal factors** that can change ovulation, and it does not alert you to seek care.

**These predictions are estimates, never diagnoses. PregnancyPal is a tracking companion, not medical advice and not a substitute for professional care. If your cycles are irregular, if you're trying to conceive or to avoid pregnancy, or if you have any concern about ovulation or fertility, please consult your healthcare provider — only a qualified clinician can assess your situation.**
