# Posture reminders

> **Posture reminders** in PregnancyPal nudge you to reset your sitting or standing position every 30–60 minutes — a small habit that often makes a noticeable difference for the back pain, sciatica, and pelvic discomfort that show up as the bump grows.

A pregnant body is rebalancing constantly. Your center of gravity moves forward, ligaments soften (thanks to relaxin), and muscles take on new loads. The result, for many people, is that sustained slouching at a desk or sustained standing at a counter gets uncomfortable fast. PregnancyPal can't change your spine, but a gentle "reset your posture" cue is one of the simplest interventions that actually helps.

## What you can do with posture reminders

- **Get a gentle reminder** every 30 or 60 minutes (your choice) during active hours.
- **See a short posture cue** with the reminder ("Roll your shoulders back. Tuck your tailbone. Plant both feet.").
- **Skip a reminder** with one tap if you've just moved.
- **Customize active hours** so reminders don't fire when you're not at a desk or counter.
- **Combine with [movement reminders](./movement) and [eye breaks](./eye-breaks)** for a single "wellness chime" rhythm.

## How to set up posture reminders

1. **Open posture reminders**: Dashboard → Wellness → Posture.
2. **Enable the reminders** with the main toggle.
3. **Pick interval** — 30, 45, or 60 minutes.
4. **Pick active hours** — typically when you're at a desk, counter, or sitting for stretches.
5. **Pick notification style** — discreet banner, with sound, or with vibration on mobile.
6. **Choose the cue rotation** — short reminders ("Reset shoulders"), longer guidance ("Plant both feet, soften your lower back…"), or specific trimester-aware cues.

## Examples

- **Desk-bound second trimester** — You sit through long meetings. The 30-minute reminder cues you to roll your shoulders back, plant both feet, and unclench your jaw. You stop noticing back tightness by the afternoon.
- **Third-trimester baking session** — You're standing at the counter for an hour. The reminder fires; you shift weight, soften your knees, and lean on the counter for a few minutes. Pelvic discomfort eases.
- **Bed-rest day** — Lying down doesn't mean posture doesn't matter. The reminder cues a small adjustment — pillows under hips, left-side preference — that keeps things comfortable longer.

## Tips

- **Plant both feet on the floor** when seated. Crossed legs or one-leg-tucked positions look comfortable but increase pelvic asymmetry over time.
- **Knees slightly below hips**, not above. A small wedge cushion or footrest can help.
- **Roll your shoulders back, then let them drop**. Bringing your shoulder blades together gently opens the chest and counters the forward-curl posture that grows with the bump.
- **Soften your lower back** — many people lock into an arched lower back as the bump grows. A small lumbar pillow helps you keep it neutral.
- **Don't overcorrect.** The goal isn't perfect posture; it's *frequent* posture. Resetting often beats holding rigid.
- **Sleep on your left side** when comfortable in the second and third trimesters. The left lateral position improves blood flow to the placenta. The right side is fine when you need a change; supine (flat on back) is best avoided for long stretches in late pregnancy.

## Frequently asked questions

### Why does pregnancy make my back hurt?

Several reasons stack: the growing bump shifts your center of gravity forward, increasing the load on the lower-back muscles; relaxin loosens ligaments (it's preparing your pelvis for birth, but it also makes joints feel less stable); and abdominal muscles weaken as they stretch, so the back picks up more of the postural work. Posture awareness is one of the most accessible interventions.

### Will posture reminders fix sciatica?

Sciatica during pregnancy often comes from baby's position pressing on the sciatic nerve, plus ligament softening. Posture alone won't "fix" it, but resetting position frequently can reduce flare-ups. For persistent sciatica, talk to your provider — physical therapy is often very helpful.

### How often should I reset my posture?

Every 30–60 minutes is the typical recommendation, and matches what most ergonomics research suggests. More frequent is fine; less frequent often means you're settling into uncomfortable positions long enough to feel them.

### Can I do stretches during the reminder?

Yes — a 20-second posture reset can absolutely include a stretch. Gentle cat-cow on hands and knees, a standing pelvic tilt, or just rolling your shoulders works. The [exercise](./exercise) and [movement](./movement) pages have more on pregnancy-safe stretches.

### Should I use a maternity belt?

Maternity belts can help some people with sciatica or pubic-symphysis pain. They're not for everyone, and the right one depends on your specific discomfort. Talk to your provider or a pelvic-floor physical therapist before relying on one daily.

### What about standing all day?

Prolonged standing can cause its own issues — swelling, varicose veins, lower-back fatigue. The posture reminder cues a weight shift, a brief sit, or a step or two regardless of your current position.

## What this feature does NOT do

- **Diagnose back pain, sciatica, or musculoskeletal issues.** Persistent or severe pain warrants a clinical visit — your provider may refer you to a pelvic-floor physical therapist.
- **Replace physical therapy.** PT is the right tool for many pregnancy-related musculoskeletal issues; reminders are an everyday complement, not a substitute.
- **Detect your actual posture.** No camera, no sensors. The reminder is time-based, not posture-aware.
- **Recommend specific products** like maternity belts or chairs.

**Severe back pain, pain that wakes you at night, or pain accompanied by numbness, tingling, or weakness in your legs warrants a call to your provider.**
