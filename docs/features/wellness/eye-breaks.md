# Eye breaks

> **Eye breaks** in PregnancyPal are gentle reminders to look away from your screen and rest your eyes — based on the **20-20-20 rule** (every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet / 6 meters away for 20 seconds) — to reduce eye strain during the long screen days that come with desk work, scrolling, and being home more.

Eye strain isn't unique to pregnancy, but pregnancy can make it more noticeable. Hormonal changes affect tear production and corneal shape, dry-eye symptoms get worse, and many people end up at home or on bed-rest with more screen time than usual. A small "look away" prompt every 20–30 minutes is a tiny intervention that has good evidence for reducing the symptom cluster known as digital eye strain.

## What you can do with eye breaks

- **Get reminded to look away** every 20 minutes (or your custom interval).
- **Get a "blink consciously" prompt** during long screen sessions (people blink ~60% less when staring at screens).
- **Toggle reminders on/off** by time-of-day so they don't fire when you're not at a screen.
- **Skip a reminder** with one tap if you're in the middle of something.
- **See a daily count** of breaks taken (optional gentle gamification).

## How to set up eye breaks

1. **Open eye breaks**: Dashboard → Wellness → Eye breaks.
2. **Enable the reminders** with the main toggle.
3. **Pick interval** — 20 / 30 / 45 / 60 minutes (default: 20 per the 20-20-20 rule).
4. **Pick active hours** — typically your work / screen hours (e.g., 9 AM – 6 PM).
5. **Pick notification style** — discreet (silent banner), with sound, or with a brief vibration on mobile.
6. **(Optional) enable the blink prompt** — fires occasionally during the active window.

## Examples

- **Desk-job pregnancy** — You work at a computer 9–5. Set the reminder window to 9 AM – 5 PM, 20-minute interval, discreet notification. You feel less afternoon eye fatigue within a week.
- **Late-pregnancy bed-rest** — You're scrolling more from the couch. Set a gentle 30-minute reminder with no sound. Each prompt is a cue to look out the window for 20 seconds.
- **Reading-heavy days** — When you read books most of the day, eye-breaks help with eye fatigue from extended near focus, not just from screens.

## Tips

- **Stand up during the 20-second look-away** when you can. It doubles as a [posture](./posture) and [movement](./movement) cue.
- **Drink water during the break.** Three wellness habits in one prompt is more sustainable than three separate reminders.
- **If you're not at a screen, dismiss the reminder.** No guilt. It's a default-on cue, not a mandate.
- **Stretch your face muscles** — slowly close your eyes, then open wide. Helps with tension that builds during focused screen time.
- **The interval is a guideline.** Some people prefer 30 minutes; the 20-minute number is the original ophthalmology-textbook recommendation, but consistency beats precision.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the 20-20-20 rule?

A widely cited recommendation: every 20 minutes of screen / near-focus work, look at something at least 20 feet (about 6 meters) away for at least 20 seconds. The intent is to relax the ciliary muscle that fatigues during sustained near focus, and to give the eye surface time to re-wet through normal blinking. The American Academy of Ophthalmology endorses the rule as a simple, evidence-supported habit for digital eye strain.

### Why does pregnancy affect eye strain?

Hormonal shifts (especially in the second and third trimesters) can change tear production and corneal hydration, making screen-related dry-eye symptoms worse. Some people experience mild refractive changes (blurriness) during pregnancy that resolve postpartum. PregnancyPal does not diagnose any of this — it just makes the "look away" habit easy.

### Will the reminders be annoying?

The default is one reminder every 20 minutes during active hours, discreet style (no sound). Most people find that imperceptible after a few days. You can shorten the window, extend the interval, or disable entirely.

### Should I use eye drops?

If you have persistent dry-eye symptoms, talk to your provider. Most preservative-free artificial tears are considered safe in pregnancy, but anything beyond that — including new prescription drops — should go through your provider. PregnancyPal does not recommend specific products.

### Does the app know when I'm at my computer?

No. PregnancyPal does not have screen-time detection. The reminder is time-based — it fires during the window you set. If you're not at a screen, dismiss the prompt.

### Can I get reminders only on weekdays?

Yes — Settings → Eye breaks → Active days. Skip weekends if your screen time is mostly weekday work.

## What this feature does NOT do

- **Diagnose vision changes** like blurriness, double vision, or floaters. Any sudden or sustained vision change during pregnancy should go to your provider — vision changes can be a symptom of preeclampsia.
- **Replace an ophthalmologist visit** if you're due for one. Pregnancy-related vision changes are usually mild and temporary, but a real exam is the only way to know.
- **Detect screen time.** The reminder is a timer, not screen-aware.
- **Replace blue-light glasses** or other physical strategies you may use.

**Sudden vision changes during pregnancy — flashing lights, dark spots, blurry vision, double vision — can be a symptom of preeclampsia and warrant a call to your provider regardless of whether you've been taking eye breaks.**
