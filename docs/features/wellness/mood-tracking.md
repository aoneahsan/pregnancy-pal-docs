# Mood tracking

> **Mood tracking** in PregnancyPal is a small daily check-in — pick how you're feeling on a simple scale, optionally add a note or tag — with a trend view that helps you notice patterns. This is a personal awareness tool. It is not a mental-health screening tool, not a diagnosis, and not a substitute for the conversations that matter when mood becomes hard.

Mood during pregnancy can swing more than usual — hormones, sleep changes, body changes, anticipation, fatigue, and life stressors stack on top of each other. Most of the ups and downs are normal. But prenatal mood conditions — antenatal depression, anxiety, intrusive thoughts — are also real, more common than people often realize, and very treatable when addressed. The mood tracker is a way to notice your own patterns; it's not a replacement for talking to your provider or a mental health professional when you need to.

## What you can do with mood tracking

- **Check in daily** with a simple mood rating (e.g., 1–5 scale, or pick from labeled options like calm / tired / anxious / happy / overwhelmed).
- **Tag what's behind it** — sleep, work, family, body changes, hormones, "no specific reason".
- **Add a short note** if you want.
- **See trends** — week-over-week, full-pregnancy view, by tag.
- **See gentle nudges** to talk to your provider if patterns suggest it's worth a conversation (these are general, not diagnostic — see the "does NOT do" section).
- **Find resources** — every page includes links to verified mental-health resources for pregnancy.

## How to log a mood check-in

1. **Open mood tracking**: Dashboard → Wellness → Mood.
2. **Pick today's mood** — 1–5 scale or labeled option.
3. **(Optional) pick tags** — what's contributing? Pick any that apply, or skip.
4. **(Optional) add a note** — anything you want to remember about today.
5. **Save**: The check-in adds to today.
6. **See the trend**: Tap the chart to see your mood over the past week, month, or trimester.

## Examples

- **Steady second trimester** — Your daily check-ins average a 4 ("good") with occasional 3s ("okay"). Tags are mostly "energy" and "appointments". The trend stays gentle.
- **Tough patch in the third trimester** — A run of 2s ("low") for two weeks, mostly tagged with "sleep" and "body changes". The app surfaces a gentle prompt: "You've been feeling low for a while. Would it be helpful to talk to your provider?" — the prompt is supportive, not alarmist, and links to mental-health resources.
- **Identifying a pattern** — You notice your mood tag "anxious" shows up most often on Sunday nights. The data doesn't tell you why, but the pattern is information you can bring to a conversation with your provider or therapist.

## Tips

- **Don't try to be "honest about being honest".** A daily mood check-in works best when you don't overthink it. Pick the first label that fits and move on.
- **Skip days when you don't want to log.** No streak pressure, no daily-completion badges. If logging itself becomes a stressor, the tool isn't helping.
- **Tags are the most useful part.** "Tired + body changes" is more informative for you and a provider than just "mood: 2".
- **Talk to your provider about your mood.** Mental health is part of prenatal care. Most providers will ask at every visit; if yours doesn't, bring it up.
- **The patterns are private to you.** Nothing about your mood log is shared, sold, or used to "train" anything. It lives in your account.

If you're struggling, you don't have to wait until your next appointment.

- **Postpartum Support International** (covers prenatal mental health too): 1-800-944-4773 (call or text) — [postpartum.net](https://www.postpartum.net/)
- **National Maternal Mental Health Hotline (US)**: 1-833-852-6262 (call or text, 24/7, free, confidential, English/Spanish)
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (US)**: call or text 988
- **Samaritans (UK)**: 116 123 (free, 24/7)
- **Your provider's after-hours line** — for many pregnant people, this is the fastest path to real support

## Frequently asked questions

### How is this different from a clinical mood scale?

It isn't a clinical scale. Validated prenatal depression and anxiety screeners (like the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale) are designed by clinicians for clinical use, with thresholds, validation studies, and provider follow-up. PregnancyPal's mood tracker is a personal-awareness tool — much simpler, designed to help you notice trends over time, not to score you against a clinical cut-off.

### Is mood-tracking helpful for everyone?

For many people, yes — noticing patterns helps. For some people, daily mood logging can become a stressor (compulsive checking, judging yourself by the log). If that's you, the tool isn't a fit. There's no harm in turning it off.

### What if my mood log shows a worrying pattern?

Talk to your provider or a mental health professional. PregnancyPal will offer a gentle prompt when patterns suggest it might be worth a conversation, but it does not screen or diagnose. Treatment options for prenatal mood conditions exist and are very effective.

### Can I share my mood log with my therapist?

Yes — export as PDF and bring it to a session, or share via email. Many therapists appreciate the trend view as a conversation starter.

### Does PregnancyPal know if I'm depressed?

No. PregnancyPal does not infer or claim any clinical state from your logs. Patterns are descriptive, not diagnostic.

### Are mood swings normal in pregnancy?

Some variability is very normal — pregnancy involves big hormonal, physical, and emotional changes. What's not "just normal" is sustained sadness, anxiety, panic, intrusive thoughts about harming yourself or the baby, loss of interest in things you usually enjoy, or persistent feelings of worthlessness or hopelessness. These warrant a conversation with your provider — they're common and treatable.

### What about postpartum?

The same tracker continues into the postpartum window. Postpartum mood conditions are common (postpartum depression affects ~15% of new mothers per CDC data) and very treatable when addressed early. Postpartum-specific resources are linked on the postpartum content page.

## What this feature does NOT do

- **Screen for or diagnose** prenatal depression, anxiety, panic disorder, OCD, or any other mental health condition. Diagnosis is a clinical task that requires a trained professional.
- **Replace your provider's mood screening.** Many providers use validated tools (EPDS, PHQ-9) at routine visits. Those screenings are designed for clinical use; the mood tracker complements them, not replaces them.
- **Provide therapy.** The app is a log, not a therapist.
- **Alert anyone.** No data leaves your account unless you export it. PregnancyPal will not contact your provider, family, or anyone else based on what you log.
- **Detect crisis.** PregnancyPal does not detect or respond to crisis statements. If you are in crisis or thinking about harming yourself, please use the resources above — those are staffed by people trained to help.

**If you're having thoughts of harming yourself or your baby, please contact a crisis line immediately. You don't have to be in crisis to ask for help — talking to your provider about mood is appropriate at any level of concern. You are not alone, and effective help is available.**
