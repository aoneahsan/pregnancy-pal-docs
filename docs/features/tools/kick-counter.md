# Kick counter

> **The kick counter** in PregnancyPal is a calm, one-tap fetal movement logger — you tap once for every kick, roll, jab, or flutter you feel, and the app records the time, session length, and pattern history so you can see if your baby's movement feels typical for them.

Fetal movement awareness is one of the simplest, most useful things a pregnant person can do in the third trimester. The kick counter is built for the moments when you're paying attention to movement — not a busy stat dashboard, just a big tap target, a session timer, and your history. It's intentionally calmer than most kick counters because the goal isn't to gamify your pregnancy; it's to help you notice your baby's pattern so you'd know if something changed.

## What you can do with the kick counter

- **Tap once for each movement** during a session — kick, roll, jab, hiccup, anything you can feel.
- **See live session info** — duration, count, time since last movement.
- **End the session manually** when you're done, or let it auto-end after 60 minutes of inactivity.
- **Browse history** — past sessions with date, duration, count, and your notes.
- **Get reminders** at times you choose (e.g., evening sessions when many people feel the most movement).
- **Export sessions as PDF** for your provider if they ask.
- **Start in two taps from the dashboard** — the kick-counter tile is in the default quick-actions row.

## How to log a kick session

1. **Open the kick counter**: From the dashboard, tap the **Log a kick** tile. The session screen takes over with a big tap target.
2. **Tap once per movement**: A clean, large button. Tapping increments the count and records the timestamp. The screen shows count, session duration, and time since last movement.
3. **Add a note (optional)**: Tap the small note icon to record context — what you were doing ("eating", "lying on left side"), how the movement felt ("strong", "subtle").
4. **End the session**: Tap **End session** when you're done. The app saves the session with start time, end time, count, and your note.
5. **Review history**: Open the history tab to see past sessions. Tap any session to see details.
6. **Set a reminder (optional)**: From the kick counter settings, choose times to remind yourself to do a session. Many people set a daily evening reminder.

## Examples

- **Daily evening session** — At 8 PM you sit down with a glass of water, open PregnancyPal, tap **Log a kick**. You feel 11 kicks over 14 minutes. You tap **End session**. The history now shows tonight's count alongside yesterday's 13 in 16 minutes — your baby's pattern looks consistent.
- **Concerned session** — Movement feels lighter than usual today. You start a session at 11 AM. After 20 minutes, you've logged only 2 kicks. The app does not raise an alert — you should call your provider yourself, because reduced movement compared to *your baby's typical pattern* is the signal that matters, not a number from an app.
- **Twin pregnancy** — With twins, distinguishing whose kick is whose isn't always possible. PregnancyPal lets you tap for any movement you feel without trying to attribute. The history is per-session, not per-baby.

## Tips

- **Pick a quiet, regular time** — eating, lying on your left side, or right after a cold drink often increases movement awareness. A consistent time builds a reliable pattern.
- **You don't need to count to a specific number.** The "count to ten" approach (used by some clinics) is one method; PregnancyPal lets you log freely so you can use whichever method your provider recommends.
- **What matters is *your* baby's pattern, not a generic threshold.** Each baby has a personal rhythm. Tracking helps you know what's normal for yours.
- **Don't panic at one quiet session.** Babies sleep too. The signal worth acting on is a meaningful, sustained change from your normal pattern — that's a call-your-provider event, not an app alert.
- **Hiccups count.** They're a great sign — rhythmic, regular, and typical of healthy fetal nervous-system development.

## Frequently asked questions

### When should I start using the kick counter?

Most providers recommend starting daily kick counts in the third trimester, around week 28. Earlier movement is often too sporadic and subtle to count reliably. Some people use the kick counter from week 18–20 just to log first kicks and watch the pattern emerge — that's fine, but the daily kick-count rhythm typically starts later.

### How long should a session be?

There's no single right answer. Two common approaches: **count to 10** (track until you feel 10 movements; note how long that took), or **time-boxed** (e.g., a 30- or 60-minute window, count whatever you feel). The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists notes that fetal movement counting methods vary; the value is consistency with whichever method your provider recommends. PregnancyPal supports both — just end the session when you've reached your target.

### What's a "normal" number of kicks?

What's normal for *your* baby is what matters. Some babies are dramatic kickers; others are quieter but consistent. After a couple of weeks of daily sessions, you'll have a personal baseline — that's the reference point. Public statistics (e.g., "10 kicks in 2 hours") are general guidance, not personal thresholds.

### Should I be worried if my baby moves less today than yesterday?

Day-to-day variation is normal. The signal worth acting on is a *meaningful, sustained* change from your typical pattern — fewer kicks across multiple sessions, or a sudden drop in movement intensity. If you're unsure, the safest action is always to call your provider. PregnancyPal will not tell you whether to call; that judgment is yours, informed by your provider's specific guidance.

### Can the kick counter detect fetal distress?

No. PregnancyPal does not interpret movement clinically. It logs what you feel and shows you the pattern. Fetal distress is diagnosed by your provider with tools like a non-stress test (NST) or biophysical profile (BPP), not by an app. The kick counter is a tracking tool, not a diagnostic.

### Does the kick counter work offline?

On native mobile (Android, iOS when published), yes. The session is saved locally and syncs when you reconnect. The web app needs a connection to save the session.

### What if I accidentally tap twice?

Tap the count number on the session screen to reveal a small minus button. Use it to subtract a count if you tapped twice or counted something that wasn't a kick (a hiccup of yours, gas, etc.). You can also delete a session entirely from the history view.

### Can I share my history with my provider?

Yes. Open kick-counter settings → "Export sessions as PDF" → choose a date range. The PDF lists each session with date, time, duration, count, and notes. Bring it to your appointment.

### What about the contractions timer?

Different tool. The [Contractions timer](./contractions-timer) is for late-pregnancy contraction tracking. Kicks and contractions are different signals; PregnancyPal keeps them separate.

## What this feature does NOT do

- **Diagnose fetal distress, decreased movement, or any clinical condition.** It records what you feel; the interpretation is between you and your provider.
- **Alert you to call your provider.** PregnancyPal will never tell you "your baby is in trouble" — apps cannot make that judgment, and pretending to is dangerous. If movement feels off, **you call your provider**, not the other way around.
- **Replace fetal monitoring.** Non-stress tests, biophysical profiles, and ultrasound are clinical tools; the kick counter is a personal tracking aid that complements (not replaces) your provider's monitoring plan.
- **Compare your baby to other babies.** PregnancyPal does not surface "average kick counts at week 32 are X." Your baby's pattern is the only reference that matters.
- **Predict outcomes.** No app can forecast a labor outcome from kick data, and PregnancyPal won't try.

**If your baby's movement feels reduced, slower, or different from their normal pattern — call your healthcare provider. Today. The kick counter is a tool to help you notice the change, not a substitute for the call.**
